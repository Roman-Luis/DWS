Roberto Clemente

One of the best Athletes and Competitors that ever lived in the game of Baseball. First Latino player to win a World Series as a starter, the year 1960.

The Best Pirate that ever lived.

Born in Carolina, Puerto Rico on August 18, 1934. Clemente was the youngest of seven siblings. He only had one sister. His father, a foreman of sugar crops. Clemente worked with his father in the same fields loading and unloading the trucks. He Joined Puerto Rico's amateur league playing for Ferdinand Juncos at the age of 16. Played in the Minor Leagues of Baseball with the Montreal Royals. At that time he was being used as a bench player and was noticed by Clyde Sukeforth, a scout for the Pittsburgh Pirates. The Pirates discussed possibilities drafting Clemente to the Pittsburgh Pirates, Clemente as the first selection of the rookie draft was selected November 22, 1954. Roberto Clemente made his debut in Major Legue Baseball with the Pittsburgh Pirates on April 17, 1955. The Pirates became the fifth team in the National League and ninth in the majors to break the baseball color line the year before, this was seven years after Jackie Robinson broke baseball's color line with the Brooklyn Dodgers. Clemente was involved in a car accident due to a drunk driver, he missed several games with an injured lower back. Finished rookie year with a batting average of .255. The year 1958 Clemente Joined the United States Marine Corps Reserve. He was a Private First Class in the Marine Corps Reserve up until September 1964. During the 1960s he led the league in many categories and got to win many awards. Clemente got to record his 3,000th hit on September 30, 1972 at Three Rivers Stadium against the New York Mets. Clemente spent much of his free time involved in charity work. On December 23, 1972 Managua, the capital city of Nicaragua had a massive earthquake. Clemente immediately tried to send out aid packages and were diverted by corrupt officials of the Somoza government. Clemente then decided to take a flight himself to ensure that the aid would be delivered to the survivors. The airplane was overloaded by 4,200 pounds and had mechanical problems and it crashed into the ocean off the coast of Puerto Rico.

Life After Death

After losing such a valuable person not only in Baseball, but in this world. There were awards given and named after him. Major League Baseball started to give out the Roberto Clemente Award in 1973, this was given to a player every year with outstanding baseball playing skills who is personally involved in community work. Clemente was presented three civilian awards of the United States government from the President of the United States including the first Presidential Citizens Medal. Babeball Writers' Association of America voted to waive the waiting period for Clemente because of the circumstances of his death.
